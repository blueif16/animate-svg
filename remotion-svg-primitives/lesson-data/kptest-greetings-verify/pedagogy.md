# Pedagogy — kptest-greetings-verify

lesson kind: language/L2

This is an L2 acquisition lesson. The child acquires three spoken English routines embedded in one continuous social encounter (meet → introduce → part). Per §9, the voice delivers the target sound and the picture delivers the moment. Reinforcement is heavy — acquisition demands multiple hearings, not one-pass coverage. The key_difficult is the /aɪm/ sound in "I'm" (§8: hardest sound gets the most repetition).

---

## Cue 1 — meet-hello

discovery:     When I meet someone, I can greet them by saying "Hello" (or "Hi") in English.
stage:         concrete — the child watches a real social moment: two kids approach, one waves and speaks.
focal:         Kid A's greeting gesture + speech bubble. The wave and "Hello!" arriving together IS the discovery — the child sees and hears what greeting looks like in English. Everything else (school gate, Kid B approaching) is quiet scene context.
reinforcement: Moderate. "Hello" is a familiar concept (the child greets in Chinese daily); the novelty is the English form. One clear model plus a choral invitation ("跟我说：Hello!") is sufficient. The teacher models, then invites the child to echo — establishing the call-and-response rhythm for the whole lesson.

---

## Cue 2 — hi-intro

discovery:     I can tell someone my name in English using "I'm ___" — and the word "I'm" is one smooth sound (/aɪm/), not "em" and not two syllables.
stage:         concrete — the child watches Kid B respond in the same encounter, modeling self-introduction as a real social act.
focal:         Kid B's response turn with the DialogueExchange emphasis flag firing on "I'm." The emphasis pulse + ReadAlongHighlight on "I'm" draws the eye and ear to the key_difficult sound within the natural phrase. The picture delivers the social moment (Kid B answering); the voice delivers the target sound.
reinforcement: Heavy — this is the lesson's key_difficult. The first exposure happens here in context (modeled BIG and slow per the brief). A dedicated follow-up cue (im-echo) provides isolated practice. The two-cue split respects §5: this cue is "hear the phrase in context"; the next cue is "drill the hard sound."

---

## Cue 3 — im-echo

discovery:     "I'm" sounds like /aɪm/ — one glide from "ah" to "m," not "em" and not "ai-m." I can copy that sound.
stage:         concrete — the child hears the isolated sound and practices producing it. Still concrete because the target is a physical mouth-sound, not a symbol or representation.
focal:         The ReadAlongHighlight displaying "I'm" with the teacher's slow, exaggerated model. The visual zooms into the speech bubble from the encounter (not a separate flashcard — a zoom-in on the moment we just watched). The sound changes: slow → normal speed → invitation to echo. The child's task is to match the sound.
reinforcement: Heavy — this is the dedicated practice beat for the hardest sound. Moves: slow model → normal-speed replay → choral invitation ("跟我说：I'm"). The teacher frames in Chinese ("这个音是 /aɪm/，一个音，不是两个") then models in English. Three exposures within one short cue. This is acquisition, not insight — it needs the repetition (§8).

---

## Cue 4 — part-goodbye

discovery:     When I leave someone, I can say "Goodbye" or "Bye-Bye" in English.
stage:         concrete — the child watches the same two kids part, completing the encounter. The wave + "Goodbye!" is a real social moment.
focal:         The parting gesture + speech bubbles from both kids. "Goodbye!" and "Bye-Bye!" arrive as the two variants, naturally showing they're interchangeable (just as "Hello" and "Hi" were in cue 1). The visual change is the kids turning/waving apart.
reinforcement: Light. "Goodbye" is phonetically straightforward for a Mandarin speaker and the child has already practiced the lesson's rhythm (model → echo). One model plus choral invitation is sufficient. The pattern is now familiar: hear it, copy it.

---

## Cue 5 — recap-encounter

discovery:     Greeting, introducing myself, and saying goodbye are three things I can do in one real English conversation — I just did all three.
stage:         concrete — the child re-sees the full encounter, retrieving each phrase as it replays. This is integration: the three separate discoveries become one social routine.
focal:         The encounter replaying in condensed form. Each phrase lights up (ReadAlongHighlight) as it plays: Hello → I'm Sam → Goodbye. The visual change is the sequential highlighting across the three moments — the child tracks which phrase comes when in the encounter.
reinforcement: This IS the reinforcement — spaced recall (§8). The child retrieves all three phrases after a short gap, which is stronger for retention than immediate repetition. No new content; just retrieval of what was learned. The teacher's narration names the structure: "打招呼、介绍自己、说再见——你会用英语交朋友了！"

---

## Reasoning notes

**Why 5 cues, not 3:** The brief's "one tiny encounter, not three flashcards" is a narrative constraint, not a pedagogical one. The encounter IS the frame, but within it, the key_difficult /aɪm/ needs its own practice beat (im-echo) separate from the phrase-in-context beat (hi-intro). This is §5 enforced: one cognitive task per beat. A 3-cue version (greet/intro/part) would cram phrase comprehension AND pronunciation drill into one beat, overloading the hardest moment.

**Why no scene-setting cue:** An 8-year-old understands "two kids at a school gate" instantly. The approach is folded into meet-hello's visual opening. A separate setup beat would violate §1 (no discovery = no beat).

**Why the recap earns its place:** Three new L2 utterances in 45–60 seconds is a lot of acquisition. Without retrieval, the child leaves having heard each phrase once or twice — not enough to own them. The recap converts three isolated discoveries into one integrated routine, and the spaced recall strengthens retention. The encounter's narrative continuity makes the recap feel like a "let's watch our friends again" rather than a drill.

**I'm /aɪm/ correction approach:** Per the brief's "do NOT correct-drill harshly" note and curriculum 应对建议, the im-echo cue models the sound warmly and invites echo — it never says "不对" or contrasts with a wrong pronunciation. The child absorbs the correct model through repetition, not error-correction.
