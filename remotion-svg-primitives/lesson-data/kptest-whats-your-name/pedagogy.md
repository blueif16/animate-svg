# kptest-whats-your-name — Pedagogy Gate (Wave 0)

The lesson teaches the English name exchange as two co‑equal spoken moves: asking "What's your name?" and answering "My name's ___." The child learns both halves as usable social routines, not isolated vocabulary. The whole lesson is ONE tiny encounter between the SAME two kids — ask → answer → role‑swap ask → role‑swap answer — so the child discovers that the question and the answer belong to *moments in a real meeting*, not to a flashcard list.

**Cognitive‑ladder note (skill §3) for a language lesson.** The three stages map to:
- **concrete** — the child watches a routine *performed in its real moment* (a kid leans in and says it at the meeting). The "object" being operated on is the social action.
- **represent** — the spoken form is surfaced as a read‑along highlight the child can track and say along with; the sound is made trackable without spelling it.
- **symbolize** — would be letters/spelling/IPA on screen. This lesson NEVER reaches stage 3: alphabet is out of scope (see brief). No cue may show a stage above `represent`.

**Match‑the‑spoken‑count rule (skill §7), language form.** When a phrase is said aloud, the on‑screen read‑along highlight must surface *that exact phrase* and no other — the moving highlight is the "count display" for sound. Especially important for the contractions: highlight `What's` as ONE swelling unit (one syllable /wɒts/), never split into two ticks; and highlight `name's` as ONE unit (the /neɪmz/ cluster), never split.

**Narration discipline (skill §4).** Teacher narration is Chinese (e.g., "听，他在问名字" / "该说自己的名字啦"): it *names the moment* (asking / answering). The English target word is *delivered by the character in the picture*, on the wave, in the bubble — the picture (the kid speaking) delivers the routine; the Chinese narration only frames the moment. If the Chinese narration spoke the English answer, the picture would be decoration.

cue: ask
discovery:  The child discovers the English question for asking someone's name: "What's your name?" — and that "What's" is pronounced /wɒts/ as one syllable, not "what is".
stage:      represent
focal:      the asking kid's mouth and the "What's your name?" speech bubble popping in on the wave (DialogueExchange turn 1); the read‑along surface "What's your name?" as it lands, with emphasis on "What's" as a single unit.

cue: answer
discovery:  The child discovers the English answer for stating one's name: "My name's ___." — and that "name's" is pronounced /neɪmz/ (the /mz/ cluster Mandarin phonology lacks), not "name is".
stage:      represent
focal:      the answering kid's mouth and the "My name's ___." speech bubble popping in on the wave (DialogueExchange turn 2); the read‑along surface "My name's ___." as it lands, with emphasis on "name's" as a single unit.

cue: ask‑swap
discovery:  The child discovers that they can be the one asking the question, using the correct pronunciation of "What's".
stage:      represent
focal:      the other kid's mouth and the "What's your name?" speech bubble (DialogueExchange turn 3); the read‑along surfaces the question as it is spoken.

cue: answer‑swap
discovery:  The child discovers that they can answer the question with their own name, using the correct pronunciation of "name's".
stage:      represent
focal:      the first kid's mouth and the "My name's ___." speech bubble (DialogueExchange turn 4); the read‑along surfaces the answer as it is spoken, with the child's name filling the blank.

cue: recap
discovery:  The child sees the whole name exchange as one connected sequence — ask → answer → role‑swap ask → role‑swap answer — confirming that both halves are usable moves in a real meeting, not isolated words to drill.
stage:      represent
focal:      the four speech bubbles lined up as the encounter's sequence (recap punctuation pulse — the lesson's second and final allowed pulse, per taste's two‑pulse rule); the two kids stay present so the arc reads as theirs.
